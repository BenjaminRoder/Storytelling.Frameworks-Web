The Hero's Journey
The universal myth cycle
Codified by Joseph Campbell · Used in: Epic fantasy, coming-of-age, adventure

I
Ordinary World
Establish the hero's normal life — their wound, desire, and the limits of their world.
Frodo lives contentedly in the Shire. His desire for adventure is small; his world is bounded by familiar hills.
II
Call to Adventure
Something disrupts the status quo. The hero is presented with a challenge or quest.
Gandalf arrives bearing news of the Ring. The outside world intrudes and cannot be ignored.
III
Refusal & Mentor
The hero hesitates. A mentor figure provides tools, wisdom, or courage to proceed.
Frodo is reluctant; Gandalf's guidance — and Bilbo's example — give him the push he needs.
IV
Crossing the Threshold
The hero commits. They leave the ordinary world behind — there is no easy return.
The Fellowship leaves Rivendell. The familiar world is gone; the adventure is real.
V
Tests, Allies, Enemies
The hero is tested. They form bonds and face opposition that reveals character.
The Fellowship encounters Moria, Lothlorien, betrayal — each test strips away illusion.
VI
The Ordeal
The darkest moment. The hero faces death — literal or symbolic — and must transform.
Frodo must walk alone into Mordor. Everything he trusted has been stripped away.
VII
Reward & Road Back
The hero seizes their prize and begins the journey home — but the danger isn't over.
The Ring is destroyed. But the Shire will need healing, and Frodo has changed forever.
VIII
Return with Elixir
The hero returns transformed, bringing something of value back to their world.
Frodo brings peace to the Shire — but carries a wound that only the Undying Lands can heal.

Best For

  • Epic and mythic narratives
  • Character transformation arcs
  • Fantasy, sci-fi, adventure
  • Long-form novels or series

Watch Out For

  • Feeling formulaic if followed too rigidly
  • Pacing sags in the middle trials
  • Overpowered mentor figures
  • Weak "return" if rushed

Writer's Prompt to Try

Your hero lives in a world where one ordinary rule has been reversed. Write the scene where they first hear their Call to Adventure — and make them refuse it for a reason the reader will respect.

1
Opening Image
A single image that captures the hero's world and emotional state before the story begins.
A woman sits alone at a banquet table set for twenty. The candles burn low. No one came.
2
Theme Stated
Someone (rarely the hero) speaks the theme aloud — casually, early, almost throwaway.
"The worst prison is the one you build for yourself," her sister says. The hero dismisses it.
3
Catalyst / Inciting Incident
The event that kicks the story into motion. Life will never be the same after this.
A letter arrives: the estate she's been managing for others is legally hers — if she claims it.
4
Break into Two
The hero makes an active choice. They step into the upside-down world of Act 2.
She boards the train. She's going to claim what's hers — for the first time in her life.
5
B Story (Heart of the Story)
A secondary plot — often a relationship — that carries the thematic weight of the story.
She meets the estate's caretaker, who asks nothing of her. This unsettles her completely.
6
Dark Night of the Soul
All is lost. The hero must die (emotionally) before they can be reborn with new understanding.
The estate burns. The claim is contested. The caretaker disappears. She has nothing and no one.
7
Break into Three
The hero synthesizes A story and B story to find the answer — and acts on the theme.
She realizes the estate was never the point. She walks back to the caretaker's cottage.
8
Final Image
Mirror of the Opening Image — but everything has changed. Shows the transformation.
She sits at a small table. Two plates. A candle. Someone knocks. She smiles and opens the door.

Best For

  • Plot-driven commercial fiction
  • Thematic precision and clarity
  • Genre fiction (thriller, romance)
  • Writers who want a scaffold

Watch Out For

  • Over-mechanizing emotional beats
  • Theme Stated feeling too obvious
  • Rigid page-count thinking
  • Neglecting the B Story's depth

Writer's Prompt to Try

Write your Opening Image and Final Image first — as a pair. They should be mirrors. Then ask: what transformation would take a character from one to the other?

Eight points on the circle — click to expand

1
You — A character in a zone of comfort
Establish who this person is and what their normal feels like.
A detective who hasn't taken a case in two years. She drinks tea and reads. She's fine. (She's not fine.)
2
Need — But they want something
The want is conscious; the need (what they truly lack) is often hidden, even from them.
She says she wants justice. She needs to believe people can be saved — including herself.
3
Go — Enter unfamiliar situation
They cross a threshold. Something forces them out of comfort.
A teenage boy knocks on her door. His mother has vanished. The case is too familiar to refuse.
4
Search — Adapt to new situation
The character tries their old tools in a new environment. Some work. Some fail.
She calls old contacts, runs old methods — but this case has a logic she doesn't understand yet.
5
Find — Get what they wanted
The surface goal is achieved — but at the midpoint, the real cost becomes visible.
She finds the mother. But the mother chose to disappear — to escape the life the boy doesn't know about.
6
Take — Pay a heavy price
Getting what they wanted costs something real. This is the story's lowest point.
To protect the boy, she has to lie to him. She has to become the thing she hates most.
7
Return — Back to familiar situation
The character returns — but the world looks different through changed eyes.
She's back in her apartment. Tea. Books. The boy sends a postcard. She puts it on the wall.
8
Changed — Having changed
The character is subtly or profoundly different. The need has been met — or exposed.
She opens her case files. She picks up the phone. She takes the next one. That's enough.

Best For

  • Character-driven literary fiction
  • Short stories and novellas
  • Series with recurring characters
  • Understanding Want vs. Need

Watch Out For

  • Flattening into a checklist
  • Forgetting the change must earn itself
  • Overusing for every subplot
  • Too neat a circle in messy stories

Writer's Prompt to Try

Write out your character's Want in one sentence and their Need in one sentence — and make them contradict each other. Then build the story from that contradiction.
04
Three-Act Structure
The classical spine
Aristotle's Poetics · The bedrock of Western narrative · Works at any length
Setup (25%)
→
Confrontation (50%)
→
Resolution (25%)

The three acts and their key turning points

I
Act One — Setup
Introduce world, character, and stakes. End with a turning point that launches Act 2.
We meet a Victorian scientist who believes she can communicate with the dead. Her patron threatens to withdraw funding unless she produces results — tonight.
→
Turning Point 1 — Inciting Incident
The point of no return. The protagonist is locked into the conflict.
During the séance, something actually answers. It knows things no living person could know.
II
Act Two — Confrontation
The protagonist pursues their goal, faces escalating obstacles, and reaches a midpoint shift.
She investigates. The entity guides her toward a murder — one that happened fifty years ago. The trail leads to her own family.
→
Midpoint — False Peak or False Collapse
Things seem won (or lost) at the midpoint — but the stakes deepen and the real conflict emerges.
She uncovers the truth of the murder — but realizes the entity has been guiding her toward a revelation she may not survive.
→
Turning Point 2 — Crisis / Dark Night
All seems lost. The protagonist must make their most difficult choice — with the highest stakes.
The entity demands payment for its help. The price is her scientific reputation — the one thing she has left.
III
Act Three — Resolution
The protagonist takes decisive action. The conflict resolves — with or without victory. The world is changed.
She publishes the truth. The world calls her a fraud. The family is exonerated. She lights a candle and waits.

Best For

  • Any genre, any length
  • First-time story architects
  • Diagnosing pacing problems
  • Subplots and nested stories

Watch Out For

  • Saggy middles (Act 2 is hardest)
  • Predictable turning points
  • Resolution that doesn't earn itself
  • Forgetting the midpoint shift

Writer's Prompt to Try

Write your Act 2 Midpoint first. What does your protagonist think they've solved — and what does the reader sense they haven't? Build Acts 1 and 3 outward from that pivot.

Brief Falling Action

The principle: no setup, only escalation

↑
Begin in Crisis
Skip the setup. Open in the middle of action or conflict. Context is revealed through crisis, not exposition.
"The car had been burning for three minutes before anyone called for help. Marlowe watched from the hill and wondered if it was already too late to pretend she hadn't seen it happen."
↑
Escalating Mini-Crises
A series of rising complications — each one harder than the last. Brief respite between, but never full resolution.
She investigates and is warned off. She digs deeper and finds a witness. The witness disappears. Her editor spikes the story. Her apartment is searched.
↑
The Major Climax
The highest, most intense crisis — where the protagonist must make the defining choice.
She's given a choice: publish and lose everything, or stay silent and live safely. She types the headline.
↓
Brief, Earned Denouement
Short — just long enough to show the consequence. The Fichtean Curve resists lingering resolutions.
Three sentences. She hits send. She closes the laptop. Outside, the city keeps moving, indifferent and alive.

Best For

  • Literary short fiction
  • Thrillers and suspense novels
  • Impatient, confident readers
  • High-stakes character studies

Watch Out For

  • Reader disorientation if too little grounding
  • Crises that don't escalate meaningfully
  • Exhausting pacing without earned rest
  • Climax that doesn't deliver on the tension

Writer's Prompt to Try

Write your story's opening line as though the reader already knows the character. Drop them into tension. Then ask: what three crises will escalate from this moment, each one stripping something away?

Nonlinear / In Medias Res
Time as a narrative tool
Latin: "into the middle of things" · Homer's Iliad · Used by Toni Morrison, Kazuo Ishiguro, Jennifer Egan
Present Crisis
↔
Past Revelation
↔
Future Consequence
↔
Emotional Truth

Key nonlinear techniques — click to explore

◆
In Medias Res Opening
Begin at a dramatic moment, then move backward to show how we got here.
Open: a character is being arrested. Chapter Two: six months earlier, a letter arrives. The reader wants to know the gap.
◆
Dual Timeline
Two timelines — past and present — that speak to each other. Each reveals what the other hides.
Present: a woman clears out her mother's house. Past: the same house, the same two women, thirty years ago. Each chapter adds a layer.
◆
Reverse Chronology
Begin at the end, move backward. Each scene recontextualizes the last. Harold Pinter's Betrayal, Martin Amis's Time's Arrow.
A marriage dissolution in chapter one becomes, in chapter twelve, the innocent moment of first meeting. The reader knows what they can't unknow.
◆
Fragmented / Mosaic Structure
Scenes, vignettes, or perspectives arranged by thematic resonance rather than chronology. Jennifer Egan's A Visit from the Goon Squad.
Twelve voices, twelve moments, different decades. The reader assembles the picture. What they assemble is part of the meaning.
◆
The Rule: Earn the Disorientation
Nonlinear structure must serve the emotional or thematic argument of the story — not just create mystique.
Ask: why does THIS story need to be told out of order? What does withholding or reordering time reveal that linear structure cannot?

Best For

  • Literary and experimental fiction
  • Memory, trauma, identity themes
  • Stories where truth is constructed
  • Unreliable narrators

Watch Out For

  • Confusion masking as complexity
  • Timelines that don't earn each other
  • Using nonlinearity as a crutch for weak plot
  • Losing the reader's emotional thread

Writer's Prompt to Try

Take a story you know well — or one you're drafting — and ask: what if the reader knew the ending from page one? How does that change every scene before it? Write the opening with the ending already given.
